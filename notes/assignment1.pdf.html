# 1. DATA INDLÆSNING MED LIMIT (Håndterer huller i data)

def fetch_sf_data(url, limit=50000):

    #Henter data. Vi tilføjer ?$limit for at sikre, at vi får mere end 1000 rækker.
    # en rigtig opgave ville man hente alt, men tror 50.000 er nok til denne opgave.
    
    full_url = f"{url}?$limit={limit}"
    response = requests.get(full_url)
    if response.status_code == 200:
        return pd.DataFrame(response.json())
    else:
        print(f"Fejl i hentning: {response.status_code}")
        return pd.DataFrame()

# Beregn  stats
total_incidents = len(df_total)
global_counts = df_total['category'].value_counts()

# Beregn ratios
districts = [d for d in df_total['district'].unique() if d not in ['NAN', 'OUT OF SF', 'UNKNOWN']]
ratios = []

for dist in districts:
    d_df = df_total[df_total['district'] == dist]
    d_total = len(d_df)
    res = {'District': dist}
    for crime in my_focus_crimes:
        p_dist = len(d_df[d_df['category'] == crime]) / d_total
        p_glob = global_counts.get(crime, 0) / total_incidents
        res[crime] = p_dist / p_glob if p_glob > 0 else 0
    ratios.append(res)

df_heatmap = pd.DataFrame(ratios).set_index('District')

plt.figure(figsize=(8, 5))
sns.heatmap(df_heatmap, annot=True, fmt=".1f", cmap='YlOrRd', cbar_kws={'label': 'Over-representation Ratio'})
plt.title('District Crime Profiles (Relative to City Average)', fontsize=16)
plt.show()

Assignment 1.1: Temporal Overview¶

Observations and Reflections:

  • Feature 1: Historical Dominance of Larceny/Theft: Looking at the plot, Larceny/Theft was significantly more frequent than other crimes between 2014 and 2018, peaking around 2017. This is likely due to San Francisco’s high volume of property crime in tourist-heavy areas.
  • Feature 2: The 2025 Spike: There is a notable and sudden spike in incidents in 2025 across almost all focus crimes. This could be explained by a change in reporting practices, a post-pandemic shift in urban activity, or perhaps a significant batch of backlogged data being integrated into the API for that year.
  • Feature 3: COVID-19 Impact (2020): Most crime types show a significant drop or stay at very low levels during 2020. This correlates with the city-wide lockdowns, which reduced street-level opportunities for crimes like Robbery and Assault.

Assignment 1.2: Crime Profiles by Police District¶

District Focus: Tenderloin

  • Pattern Description: The heatmap reveals that the Tenderloin district has a massive over-representation of Drug/Narcotic offenses, with a ratio of 2.3 (or as high as 9.3 in some data views) compared to the city average.
  • Explanation: This pattern is likely driven by both geographic and demographic factors. The Tenderloin has historically been a hub for social services and low-income housing, but it is also an area where open-air drug markets have been more visible. Consequently, police enforcement for narcotics is highly concentrated in this specific district compared to residential areas like Richmond or Taraval.

Assignment 1.3: Visualizing Distributions¶

Part A — Jitter Plot Reflections:

  • The jitter plot reveals clear vertical "strips" at the 0, 15, 30, and 45-minute marks. This suggests that incident times are often rounded to the nearest quarter-hour by reporting officers rather than being recorded with second-by-second precision.

Part B — Probability Plot Reflections:

  • The points in the QQ-plot deviate significantly from the red reference line, forming an "S-shape". This indicates that the latitude of crimes is not normally distributed; instead, crime is clustered in specific latitudinal bands (specific neighborhoods), meaning the geography of crime in SF is highly localized rather than being spread out like a bell curve.

Part C — Time-of-day (Violin Plot) Reflections:

  • Fraud shows a concentration during business hours (10:00–16:00), whereas Robbery and Assault have wider distributions extending into the late-night hours.
  • Midnight Wrap-around: Standard plots struggle with the transition from 23:59 to 00:00, often showing a "gap" at the bottom and top of the Y-axis. In reality, these times are continuous, but numerically they are treated as opposites (0 vs 23), which can mislead the viewer about the continuity of nighttime crime.

Assignment 1.4: Spatial Power Law¶

Observations and Reflections:

  • The log-log plot shows a roughly linear relationship between the number of incidents ($k$) and the number of cells ($N(k)$). This suggests that SF crime follows a spatial power law, where a vast majority of grid cells have very little crime, while a tiny handful of "hotspots" account for the majority of incidents.
  • Implication: This means that the "average" crime rate for a neighborhood is often misleading. If one block in a neighborhood is extremely dangerous and the other 49 blocks are safe, the "average" will make the whole area look moderately dangerous, which is not an accurate reflection for most residents.

Assignment 1.5: Regression and Correlation¶

Analysis of Results:

  • Most Correlated: Pairings like Assault and Robbery often show the highest Pearson R values (e.g., $R > 0.60$). This matches intuition because both are "street crimes" that thrive in the same environment and at similar times (usually late night/weekends).
  • Least Correlated: Fraud and Vandalism often show lower correlation. This is logical as Fraud is often a non-physical, daytime activity, while Vandalism is frequently a crime of opportunity occurring under the cover of darkness.
